We stopped asking teachers "how did it go?"

Then we taught the AI to quote their own classroom back to them — to the second.

Rumi coaches teachers by listening to a recording of their lesson and then asking a few reflective questions over WhatsApp. For months those questions were polite and generic — the kind a teacher learns to pattern-match by her third session. How did the lesson go? What would you change?

The answers told the story. They were getting shorter. The share of teachers who walked away with a concrete "next time I will…" plan was sliding. A good coach never asks the same question twice — and never asks one a teacher can answer on autopilot. Our AI coach was doing both.

So in May 2026 we rebuilt the question engine from scratch. The rule for the new version was simple: never ask a question the teacher could have answered without having taught that exact lesson.

A generic question gets a generic answer. We wanted the AI to put the teacher back inside the one minute of her lesson that actually mattered. — Design principle, Coaching v12
One classroom · one question · three ways

The same moment, asked three different ways

Ehtisham's maths lesson, 20 May. The AI noticed a contradiction: at 05:00 the children were counting with their own hands; by 11:20 he had handed them a rule — "always start from the unit." Asked to write a reflective question about that turn, the engine produced three genuinely different ones. None is a template.

Variation A — the counterfactual
05:00 پر بچے لائنز کھینچ کے گن رہے تھے — اپنے ہاتھوں سے سمجھ رہے تھے۔ پھر 11:20 پر اچانک ایک رول آیا کہ ہمیشہ یونٹ سے سٹارٹ کرو۔ ان دو لمحوں کے بیچ میں کیا ہوا جو آپ نے رخ بدلا؟
"At 05:00 the children were counting with their hands — understanding it themselves. Then at 11:20 a rule appeared: always start from the unit. What happened between those two moments that made you change direction?"
Variation B — the learner's mind
ایک ہی لیسن میں دو الگ طریقے — 05:00 پر "خود گنو" اور 11:20 پر "رول یاد رکھو"۔ آپ کے خیال میں بچے کے ذہن میں یہ شفٹ کیسی محسوس ہوئی ہو گی؟
"Two different methods in one lesson — 'count it yourself' at 05:00 and 'remember the rule' at 11:20. How do you think that shift felt inside the child's mind?"
Variation C — the hypothetical student
آپ نے 11:20 پر بچوں سے کہا "ہمیشہ رائٹ سے۔" 05:00 پر آپ نے انہیں خود ایڈیشن دریافت کرنے دیا تھا۔ اگر کوئی بچہ پوچھے کہ "میں نے تو خود گن کر کر لیا تھا، رول کیوں؟" — تو آپ کیسے بتائیں گی؟
"At 11:20 you told them 'always from the right.' At 05:00 you'd let them discover addition themselves. If a child asked, 'but I worked it out on my own — why the rule?' — how would you answer?"

All three cite the real timestamps from her actual lesson, surface the same teaching moment, and avoid advice or judgement entirely. But they read like three different thoughtful coaches. Variation C even invents a student's voice — something a fill-in-the-blank template categorically cannot do.

How the AI finds the moment

Before writing anything, the engine reads the whole transcript and looks for a contradiction — two moments on the same thread where the teaching changed. That tension becomes the question.

05:00
Children counting with their hands — discovering it themselves.
11:20
A procedural rule arrives: "always start from the unit."

What happens after the recording

1
Read the whole lesson. One pass extracts the through-line, the significant moments, and the moments where a child's answer revealed their thinking.
2
Notice (Q1). Quote one real moment back to the teacher, naming the child if we can verify the name, and place her inside it.
3
Listen, then adapt (Q2). The next question reacts to what she actually answered — not a script — moving to a different moment on the same thread.
4
Commit (Q3). It closes on her own first thought for next time she meets that specific kind of moment — a plan, in her words.

The rules a question must survive

Eight checks run automatically after the AI writes each question. Fail one, and it's rewritten — up to three times — before a teacher ever sees it.

01
Must stay under the cognitive-load ceiling — no rambling.
02
Cannot open with a yes/no stem (did / was / can / کیا) — open inquiry only.
03
No advice wearing a question mark — no should, try, chahiye.
04
Must be anchored to real evidence — a timestamp or the teacher's own words.
05
Must reference what was done, never who the teacher is — no character judgement.
06
The closing question must name a cue, a moment, and an observable action.
07 · 08
Must be genuinely new — checked against the teacher's last sessions by fingerprint and meaning.

The question is now a voice note — and it speaks Arabic without being taught.
